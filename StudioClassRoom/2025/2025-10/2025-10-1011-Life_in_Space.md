# Life in Space

Don't take **gravity** for granted!

by David Morton

Many people consider it exciting to travel to space, often imagining the fancy images of stars and planets from **science-fiction** (sci-fi) movies or expensive commercial spaceflights. But actually, living in space is more difficult, dangerous and messy than those pictures let on.

Living off-world in a microgravity (almost zero-gravity) environment requires careful planning and training. Astronauts and scientists on the International Space Station (ISS) can't jump into a shower or take a relaxing bath. Instead, they have to rely on washing with a cloth and using special shampoos to stay clean. Even using the bathroom becomes a difficult process, involving bags, tubes and toilets designed to **prohibit** waste from floating around.

Eating is another adventure. Without gravity to hold food in place, food and liquids float in all directions, which can damage **equipment**. Astronauts eat most of their meals from sealed containers and drink liquids from special bags. While these solutions are clever, the menu is often limited. Eating the same meals every day for weeks or months can lead to a loss of appetite, which can cause serious problems like weight loss or poor **nutrition**.

1. The ISS circles Earth about every 90 minutes. That means astronauts see 16 sunrises and 16 sunsets every single day!
2. Astronauts recycle their "number one." The ISS has a special system that recycles astronauts' waste into clean drinking water.
3. The station is huge — about the size of a football field. It's the largest human-made structure in space and can be seen from Earth with the naked eye.
4. Bread is banned in space. Pieces of bread can float into machines and cause problems!
5. Over 3,000 experiments have been done aboard the ISS so far. Their discoveries are making a difference for people on Earth.

Living off-world takes a toll on astronauts' bodies in other ways as well. Liquids in the body, like blood, move toward the head instead of flowing throughout the astronauts' bodies, often resulting in puffy faces and sometimes vision problems. Additionally, staying **physically** fit in space requires much more effort than it does on Earth. Without the pull of gravity to resist their movements, astronauts' muscles and bones start to weaken over time. So, NASA recommends that astronauts exercise for about two hours each day using machines that are designed for training in microgravity.

Despite these hardships, the unique environment of this floating **laboratory** gives scientists a chance to study things in a way that's impossible on the ground. Cancer research, for example, has received a major boost from ISS studies. In these weightless conditions, scientists have been able to grow and study **blood vessel** cells. This research enables them to test new drugs that could block a **tumor**'s ability to grow its own blood supply.

Space travel looks amazing from the outside, but behind every photo are real challenges. Thanks to astronauts' daily efforts, science moves forward, and life on Earth grows a little better each day.

## Key Points
- Living in space looks exciting, but the reality involves floating food and limited mealtimes.
- The experiments that astronauts perform in the ISS laboratory can lead to medical discoveries that benefit people on Earth.

## Usage Tip
### Tip 1
S + consider + it + OC (adj./N) + to + VR

本句型採用「動詞 + 受詞 + 受詞補語」的結構，動詞 consider 表「認為」，後方使用虛受詞 it，再接上受詞補語（以形容詞或名詞呈現），說明真正的 to VR 這個行為。能套用本句型的動詞除 consider 外，還包含 deem, find, think, believe 等動詞。例句：Teachers believe it to be their duty to inspire students' curiosity.

### Tip 2
S + ask/demand/request/order/suggest/advise/recommend/propose (that) S + VR/(should) VR

能運用於本句型之動詞，均含有「建議、要求、命令」的意味；其後接 that 子句，表提議或要求的內容，其中 that 可省略。在此句型中，無論該 that 子句中的主詞是單數或複數，子句中的動詞一律使用原形，抑或是可加上助動詞 should，以強化建議或命令語氣。例句：The museum asks (that) every visitor (should) be checked for prohibited items.
