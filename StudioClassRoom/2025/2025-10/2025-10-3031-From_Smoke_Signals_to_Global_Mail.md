# From Smoke Signals to Global Mail

How our modern postal system came to be

by Christine Bednari

Imagine a world without texts or emails. That's hard to picture when we rely on them every day! But even ancient civilizations had ways to share information across great distances. Smoke signals were used by **Aboriginal** Australians as far back as 15,000 B.C. Drums that copied speech sent messages between villages in Mesopotamia and South Asia by 6000 B.C.

As civilizations grew, they built better message systems using couriers. Egypt had the first postal system around 2400 B.C. In 1000 B.C., China improved delivery with a **relay** system, using stations with fresh horses to carry messages up to 200 miles a day. Persia pushed the envelope around 600 B.C., building roads from Turkey to India and using one language for messages. Their expert riders cut a three-month trip on foot to just over a week. These postal systems carried only royal or government **correspondence**.

By the Middle Ages, mail systems weren't just for royals or the government. Businesses created their own networks, like the *Metzgerpost*, which used **butchers** to carry letters along their routes.

In the 1840s, people had trouble handling mail with rockets. One rocket opened 3,000 letters, but rocket mail never quite took off.

Pigeons have carried messages for centuries, particularly in times of war. One pigeon named 888 was given the rank of colonel in the U.S. Army and was buried with honors when he died.

Today's **postal** systems owe much to the Thurn and Taxis family, whose postal empire lasted from 1290 to 1867. At their peak, they had 20,000 messengers delivering mail far and wide across Europe. Their royal-turned-public postal service helped **establish** the world standard.

Two **innovations** in the 1800s helped shape modern postal systems. First, British school principal Rowland Hill invented the postage stamp and proposed prepaid mail priced by weight. Before that, the **recipient** had to pay the fee; if they didn't, the letter would not be delivered. England adopted the stamp quickly, and the Thurn and Taxis postal system followed. Second, the Universal Postal Union (UPU) was created in 1874 to **standardize** international rates and regulations.

With cheaper postage and a simpler system, mail delivery expanded — sometimes in strange ways. In 19th-century London, mail was delivered 12 times a day! And in early 20th-century America, some parents mailed their children to relatives because it was cheaper than train fare! One baby was sent one mile to his grandmother's house for 15 cents!

During World War II, British and American families used V-Mail. Letters were photographed, **condensed** into film rolls and printed at their **destinations**.

By the mid-1900s, postal codes expedited sorting and improved accuracy. Today, email has mostly replaced snail mail, but package delivery is booming.

Postal systems specialize in tracked deliveries and machine or AI sorting. Japan is testing self-driving delivery robots, and Rwanda uses drones to fly medical supplies to remote villages.

The mail keeps moving — shaped by people's needs, ideas and the unique ways we stay in touch.

## Key Points
- Early civilizations created clever ways to send messages, starting with royal relay systems that delivered government correspondence, and later becoming public and worldwide.
- Innovations such as postage stamps, standardized international rules and new technology improved postal systems.

## Usage Tip
### Tip 1
S + owe much to + N

動詞 owe 意為「欠債；虧欠」，用於 owe A to B 這片語中，乃「將 A 歸功於 B」之意，其中 A 為某個正面結果，B 則是達成結果的貢獻者，通常以名詞形式出現作為介系詞 to 的受詞。本句中的 A 為 much，表「許多成就；這般成就」，亦可替換為 a lot 或 a great deal。例句：The company owes its reputation to its commitment to quality.

### Tip 2
If + S + V-ed, S + would/could/might + VR

該句型可不用於與現在事實相反的假設語氣，而是單純敘述過去某條件成立時會發生的結果，常用於說明過往的規則或習慣。因情境是發生在過去，所以使用過去式助動詞 would/could/might + VR。例句：In the past, if the teacher was absent, class might be canceled; but now, lessons can be served online instead.
