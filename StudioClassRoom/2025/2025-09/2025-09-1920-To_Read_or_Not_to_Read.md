# To Read or Not to Read

Why reading matters more than ever in the digital age

by Linda Ludwig

Studies show that reading strengthens a person's mental capacity, supports language learning and may even contribute to a longer, healthier life.

How many times a week do you pick up a book to read for pleasure? With so many available distractions, it might be better to ask how many times a month you read a book. The digital age, with streaming apps, social media platforms and online content being so readily accessible, has indeed changed reading habits, encouraging everyone to consume bite-sized pieces of information.

However, numerous reasons for us to read extend far beyond enjoyment, including being crucial for our brains. An Emory University study measured MRI scans on people on nine mornings after they had read a section of a novel the previous evening. The findings showed that the deeper they were immersed in the story, the more areas of their brain were active. Researchers from Boston Children's Hospital found that reading creates new connections between brain cells and strengthens the communication between the brain's two hemispheres, helping people learn faster. Furthermore, reading enhanced people's auditory and visual comprehension.

If that isn't enough, a University of Sussex study discovered that when completely involved in a book for as little as six minutes, people experienced reduced blood pressure and a reduced heart rate. Also, their muscles began to relax. Research recommends 30 minutes of daily reading to dramatically diminish stress symptoms.

Nowadays, people find their attention being pulled in a thousand different directions at once. Within five minutes, the average person is working on a task, checking their email, chatting on social media, monitoring their phone and interacting with those around them. This minimizes one's ability to focus and concentrate; in contrast, being absorbed in a good book heightens those abilities.

A plus for readers is that they enhance their literacy whenever they encounter and learn unfamiliar words. This in turn enables them to communicate more effectively. It's not surprising that reading is vital for language learners as exposure to words used in context helps them improve their language skills.

Reading also teaches compassion as readers often find themselves relating to the narrator, which helps them understand and share someone else's feelings. Gaining different perspectives of the world also helps people connect better with others.

A Yale University study of non-readers, people who read less than 3.5 hours a week and those who read more than that both groups of readers lived longer than the non-readers. Furthermore, it showed that readers maintained their cognitive functioning, helping them recall information more easily.

Reading remains a powerful tool that will help us grow as individuals and keep our minds sharp.

## Key Points

- Reading not only strengthens one's cognitive function and learning ability but also reduces stress, improves comprehension and enhances overall health.
- An essential tool for personal literacy and mental well-being, reading improves a person's focus, communication and memory function.

## Usage Tip
### Tip 1
`as little as`

`as little as` 用來描述數量、時間等少得令人意外的程度，常譯為「少到僅有……」。例句：As few as 20 trees were left in the forest after the wildfire.

### Tip 2
`It's hardly/scarcely/not surprising (that) ...`

本句型以虛主詞 `It` 開頭，說明某個情況「並不讓人意外」。其中 `hardly` 和 `scarcely` 除了表示「幾乎沒有」之外，也可作為副詞加強語氣，表「一點也不讓人意外」。例句：It's hardly surprising (that) lowering the fuel price will lead to more people driving.
