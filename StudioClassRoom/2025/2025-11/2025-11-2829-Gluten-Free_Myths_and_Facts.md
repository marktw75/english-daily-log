# Gluten-Free: Myths and Facts

The benefits and limitations of a gluten-free diet

by Elizabeth Koroma

People with certain medical conditions benefit from avoiding gluten, but most people only benefit from avoiding gluten if it helps them choose food that is healthy for other reasons.

Gluten, a protein found in wheat and a handful of other grains, often gets blamed for everything from bloating to brain fog. As more people are ditching it for what they claim are health reasons, gluten-free products are flooding grocery aisles and myths are multiplying. But is giving up gluten really a health fix or just hype? Let's sift through the fiction and uncover the truth of this trend.

## Myth 1: Gluten Is Bad for You

The vast majority of people can eat gluten with no ill effects, but some people's bodies cannot tolerate it. The most well-known reason for this is celiac disease, a condition in which gluten harms the lining of the small intestine, leading to issues with digestion, stomach pain and fatigue. People with celiac disease need to follow a gluten-free diet to remain healthy. There are also people with non-celiac gluten sensitivity who may experience symptoms similar to those with celiac disease after eating gluten but don't actually have the disease.

## Myth 2: A Gluten-Free Diet Is a Low-Carbohydrate Diet

Since what is high in carbohydrates, it's easy to assume a gluten-free diet would be low in the substance. However, many gluten-free foods, such as rice, are still high in carbohydrates. Thus, a gluten-free diet does not necessarily mean the diet will be low in carbs.

## Myth 3: It's Easy to Tell Which Foods Are Gluten-Free

Prepackaged foods labeled gluten-free are easy to recognize, but don't assume any other food without wheat is gluten-free unless the label directly says so. Also, if a restaurant cooks gluten-free food using the same pans and utensils as it does for other food, small amounts of gluten may contaminate the gluten-free offerings. While small amounts may not affect people with mild gluten sensitivity, they can pose serious risks for individuals with celiac disease and lead to serious side effects.

## Myth 4: A Gluten-Free Diet Will Make You Feel Better and Lose Weight

Some people insist that gluten-free diets are beneficial for everyone, but this is not the case. For people with celiac disease or non-celiac gluten sensitivity, avoiding gluten helps relieve their symptoms and improve their well-being. Those with wheat allergies can also benefit since avoiding gluten often means avoiding wheat. But for people without those conditions, the impact of this kind of diet depends entirely on what replaces the gluten. Replacing cookies with fresh fruit and vegetables may lead to weight loss and more energy, but simply choosing gluten-free cookies instead of regular ones is unlikely to have any benefit.

In and of itself, avoiding gluten does not make a diet healthy, but following a gluten-free diet can be invaluable for those who truly need it.

## Key Points

- Gluten is a protein found in wheat that is not bad for everyone, but some people have negative reactions to it.
- Gluten-free diets are important for people who can't tolerate gluten, but for others, they are only helpful if foods with gluten are replaced with healthier options.
