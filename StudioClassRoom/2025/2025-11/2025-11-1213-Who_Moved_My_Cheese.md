# Who Moved My Cheese?

Adapt to change - or be left behind

summarized by Simon Stopps

Life doesn't always go the way we expect it to. Dr. Spencer Johnson's *Who Moved My Cheese?* presents a simple story that illustrates a powerful truth: change is constant and our response shapes our success. The book's characters, two mice, Sniff and Scurry, and two tiny people, Hem and Haw, live in a maze and survive by finding cheese. But when their regular supply disappears, each of them reacts differently.

Sniff and Scurry waste no time. They quickly head out in pursuit of new cheese. Caught off guard, Hem and Haw struggle. Hem resists change. He's angry and afraid, hoping the cheese will come back. Haw, however, analyzes his fear. He wonders if staying still is more dangerous than moving forward. If he had left earlier, he might have found new cheese sooner. This is where the story shifts.

The cheese is a symbol for anything people want - a job, a relationship or success. Johnson uses this parable to explore how people often cling to the past even when it no longer serves them. Those who succeed are usually those willing to move on. Haw eventually learns that moving on is the first step toward growth.

Haw begins writing messages on the maze's walls to capture what he's learning. One of his first notes says, "If you do not change, you can become extinct." That idea is central to the book. Johnson believes that change is not just possible - it is necessary. Life will keep moving whether we like it or not, and those who stay flexible are more likely to thrive.

Another message Haw writes is: "What would I do if I weren't afraid?" This question helps him take action. Even though he is still reluctant, he starts looking for new cheese. It isn't until he begins taking action that he starts to feel more confident. His mindset changes. Haw realizes that the fear he had wasn't helpful - it was holding him back. When he finally finds a new source of cheese, he's not just relieved - he's wiser.

*Who Moved My Cheese?* is short, but its lessons are lasting: Don't let fear trap you. Stay alert. Notice changes. Adapt quickly. Johnson reminds us that while we can't always control what happens, we can control how we respond. And often, that makes all the difference.

## Key Points

- Spencer Johnson tells a simple story that illustrates change is constant, but our response shapes success.
- The story of Hem and Haw teaches us that while we can't always control what happens, we can control how we respond.

## Usage Tip

`It is not until ... that ...` 常用來強調某件事直到某個時間點或條件出現後才發生。  
例句：It isn't until he begins taking action that he starts to feel more confident.
