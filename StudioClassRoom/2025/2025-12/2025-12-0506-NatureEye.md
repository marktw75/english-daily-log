# NatureEye

Exploring the world without leaving a footprint

NatureEye enables people to see some of Earth's most beautiful natural landscapes through the lens of a drone that is controlled remotely.

For those driven by a desire to see the world but limited by time or budget, a new form of exploration has begun to emerge. Since its launch in October 2023, NatureEye has offered users the rare chance to remotely fly a drone in real-time over some of the planet's most spectacular and protected ecosystems. Whether it's soaring over the volcanoes of Iceland or drifting across the wild landscapes of Kenya, the drone allows participants to experience natural wonders without contributing to the harmful effects of overtourism.

The concept, which was conceived during the COVID-19 pandemic, captures a shift in how people think about travel. While many people expected a full return to in-person tourism, a growing number have recognized that virtual travel is not simply a temporary substitute, but a practical alternative.

Each 30-minute NatureEye session includes a briefing and the option to control the drone's camera. Users are assisted by an expert guide - sometimes a professional photographer - who not only explains the area's significance but also helps visitors capture beautiful images from a unique perspective. Prices start at US$95 per flight and up to 50 percent of the revenue goes directly to local conservation sites, meaning that whoever purchases a flight participates in conservation efforts.

For schools, the platform has proven to be a game-changer. With real-time lessons and drone-guided field trips, students can explore complex topics like climate science, wildlife conservation and cultural heritage. Through these lessons, students become acquainted with drone technology, real-time data collection and environmental care - skills that are central to modern STEM education. But the benefits of this technology to power tourism extend far beyond the classroom.

Overtourism has become more than just an annoyance - it is a growing crisis. Abuse of local infrastructure, destruction of natural habitats and increasing levels of waste and carbon emissions have all ben linked to too many visitors. In accordance with growing calls for responsible tourism, NatureEye presents a reasonable compromise. While it may not fully replicate the emotional depth of visiting the Elephant Sands of Botswana, its ability to make exploration accessible without exploitation sets this experience apart.

If we wish to preserve Earth's most precious places, we may need to amend not just how we travel, but why. NatureEye invites us to view exploration not as a physical conquest, but as an experience that can be shaped by technology, curiosity and care. By doing so, it may help diminish the pressure we place on the natural world while still allowing us to delight in its wonders.
