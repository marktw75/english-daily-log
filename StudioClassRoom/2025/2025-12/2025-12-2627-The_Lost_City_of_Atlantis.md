# The Lost City of Atlantis

Is this myth based on fact?

The great ancient Greek philosopher Plato wrote of an island city-state ruled by wise and powerful kings. But over time, the rulers of this city turned away from virtue, becoming proud and self-serving to the point of waging war on their former ally, Athens. According to Plato, this pride eventually angered the gods so much that they sent fire and earthquakes, causing the island to sink into the ocean. This city, called Atlantis, has captured people's imaginations for centuries.

Until the 19th century, most people assumed that Atlantis was a mythical place, but in 1882, Ignatius Donnelly published a book arguing that Atlantis was a real place. He claimed that it was the source of all the major discoveries of ancient civilization, such as agriculture, language and metallurgy. Advances in science have disproven his theory, but the idea that Atlantis may have been a real place persists.

A more recent theory links Atlantis with the Minoan civilization, which once flourished on the islands of Crete and Thera. Four thousand years ago, the Minoans built magnificent palaces, paved roads and developed one of the first writing systems in Europe, called Linear A. But an earthquake triggered a volcanic eruption and a series of tsunamis that destroyed this once-great civilization. While the geographic location of the Minoan civilization does not match what Plato says of Atlantis, the other parts of the story fit.

The majority of scholars today believe that Atlantis was not a real place at all. They say that Plato invented it as a moral lesson about pride, ethics, not history. Plato may have been inspired by real places and events since many great cities throughout history were destroyed by natural disasters, but Atlantis itself exists only in Plato's writings. After all, Plato says that Atlantis' conflict with Athens and subsequent destruction took place thousands of years before him, long before Athens even existed.

But even accepting the view that Atlantis was not necessarily a real place, the story is still important. It serves as a warning: A good beginning does not necessarily ensure ongoing success. In fact, success can lead to arrogance, which in turn can lead to ruin. Humility and virtue are more important than power, which is why in Plato's story the smaller, weaker city of Athens grows into a great power while Atlantis is buried.

Much of the fascination with Atlantis comes from the fact that it is a story that appeals to the imagination. But the deeper value lies in the timeless lessons Plato's writings offer if it teaches about pride, power and the fall of great civilizations.

### Fun Fact

Atlantis and the Atlantic Ocean share a root word: Atlas. In modern English, an atlas is a map. While an atlas is 2-D, a globe is 3-D. A globe is a sphere (a ball) that has a map of the earth on it!

## Key Points

- Plato's tale of Atlantis may be mythical, but some believe it reflects the real Minoan civilization, which was destroyed by a powerful volcanic eruption and earthquakes.
- Though likely fictional, the story of Atlantis offers timeless lessons about pride, power and how arrogance can lead even great civilizations to ruin.
