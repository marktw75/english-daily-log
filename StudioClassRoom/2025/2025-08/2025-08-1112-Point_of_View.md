# Point of View (POV)

A critical element in writing and reading

by Linda Ludwig

Point of view (POV) shapes how a story is told and experienced. Whether writing fiction or nonfiction, a person's point of view can influence a reader's emotions, understanding and interest. It also determines the reader's connection to the characters, their depth of insight into events and the overall tone of the story. The three points of view most often used in writing are first person, second person and third person.

First-person POV uses the perspective of "I" or "we," providing an intimate, personal view of the story. By experiencing events through the eyes of the storyteller, the reader gets a deeper look into a character's mind. This style creates a strong connection with the narrator but may limit the reader's understanding of other characters and events. A good example is Moby-Dick, in which readers experience the story through the eyes of Ishmael, one of the ship captain's crewmates.

Second-person POV employs the less common perspective of "you" to involve the reader. It is often used in self-help books as well as some types of fiction. The children's novel Choose Your Own Adventure use this POV to actively engage readers.

Third-person POV adopts the perspective of "he," "she" or "they" and can be either limited or omniscient. Newspaper articles, which are most often written in third person, are a good example.

Third-person limited POV focuses on one character's thoughts and experiences at a time. Plots with suspense, such as Murder on the Orient Express by Agatha Christie, commonly use this method.

Third-person omniscient POV provides insight into multiple characters, allowing a broader view of the story. Classic novels like Pride and Prejudice by Jane Austen use this style to explore different characters' thoughts.

Choosing the right POV significantly affects storytelling. For readers, understanding POV helps them interpret how biased a story is. A first-person writer may give a very personal account, while a third-person omniscient writer gives a fuller picture. Writers use POV to shape engagement, suspense and emotional depth.

Point of view is a powerful tool that influences both storytelling and reader experience. By better understanding POV, writers can control how stories develop, and readers can better appreciate the differences in writers' perspectives.

## Key Points

- When writing, a person's point of view, which includes first, second and third-person perspectives, influences the reader's connection, emotions and tone.
- Point of view determines storytelling style and bias, which affect the reader's experience.

## Usage Tip
### Tip 1
`N prep. + which/whom + S + V ...`

關係代名詞 `which` 或 `whom` 可指前方之 `N`，引出形容詞子句對 `N` 加以修飾。若該關代（即該 `N` 在形容詞子句中當介系詞的受詞），則可把介系詞移至關代前，使句子更正式。如本句之形容詞子句要表達 `experience` 這動作是發生在「Moby Dick 這本小說裡」，故可將介系詞 `in` 置於代指 `Moby Dick` 的 `which` 前。例句：I contacted the travel agent through whom I booked a tour to Japan.

### Tip 2
`Most often` vs. `more often than not`

頻率副詞 `often`（經常）的最高級說法為 `most often`，表「最常……」，因此本句即表示新聞報導最常用第三人稱視角寫成。而與 `often` 比較級相關的片語為 `more often than not`，就字面上來看，是在表達「某事的發生比起不發生更為經常」，因此即為「多半，通常」之意。例句：More often than not, the student is 10 minutes late for school.
