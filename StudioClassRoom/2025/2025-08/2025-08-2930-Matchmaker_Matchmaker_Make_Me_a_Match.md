# Matchmaker, Matchmaker, Make Me a Match

A little help to find the perfect match

by Ruth DeVine

Matchmaking, in all its forms, shows that finding true love is a universal pursuit across cultures.

Once upon a time, long before dating apps and reality TV shows promised to help people find their "perfect match," love was often a community affair. Families, religious leaders and professional matchmakers played a crucial role in bringing people together. Whether in ancient China, medieval Europe or 19th-century America, matchmaking has always been about more than just romance. It was about survival, social status and securing the future.

Matchmaking was performed by a person, usually an older woman. In ancient China, no matchmakers, known as mei ren (媒人), occupied a respected place in society. They arranged marriages based on astrology, family status and compatibility. Parents rarely left their children's futures to chance, believing that love could grow from a well-arranged partnership. Young people were thought not to have the maturity to navigate the difficulties of choosing the right partner. A matchmaker knew the best options available to a young man or woman.

In Europe marriage was often a business transaction rather than a love story, and the upper class used marriage to form political alliances, which matchmakers were only too happy to have a hand in. Meanwhile, commoners relied on local matchmakers or church leaders to help them find suitable spouses. By the 19th century, personal ads in newspapers began appearing, allowing individuals to take a more active role in finding partners.

The 20th century brought drastic changes. The rise of cities, industrialization and women's independence led to the decline of traditional matchmaking. As people moved away from small communities and family influence, they turned to social clubs, dance halls and, eventually, dating agencies to meet potential partners.

Fast-forward to today, when technology has taken the lead in modern romance. Dating apps like Coffee Meets Bagel, Goodnight and SweetRing have revolutionized how people meet. Instead of relying on family or community connections, singles now turn to algorithms, AI and personality quizzes to find compatible partners.

But matchmaking hasn't gone completely digital. Elite matchmaking services cater to wealthy individuals looking for a tailored, old-school approach. Companies like Kelleher International and Selective Search operate much like historical matchmakers, carefully vetting clients and setting up personalized dates. They boast high success rates and charge premium fees.

Reality TV has made matchmaking a spectacle. Shows like Indian Matchmaking are bringing the ancient art of matchmaking into the entertainment world. The show's success proves that even in the digital age, people are still fascinated by the search for love.

Whether through ancient tradition or modern technology, one thing remains true: humans desire connection. Matchmaking, in all its forms, shows that finding true love is a universal pursuit across cultures.

## Key Points

- Matchmaking has historically been a community affair that focused on compatibility, status, survival and love rather than just romance.
- Matchmaking has changed from community-based methods to dating apps and elite services, but the human desire for connection remains unchanged.

## Usage Tip
### Tip 1
`Only too happy to vs. too happy to`

片語 `only too happy to V` 表「很樂意，極度願意如此」，語意比 `too V` 更積極，也較固定搭配，`too happy to V` 則較少見，通常不如 `only too happy to V` 自然。此處描述上流階級的媒人很樂意促成政治婚姻。例句：The teacher was only too eager to praise her students' work.

### Tip 2
`N + when-clause`

關係副詞 `when` 引導修飾 `N` 的子句，功能大致上等於「介系詞 + which」，若 `N` 為時間點（如本句的 `age` 或 `20th century`），使用 `when` 會更自然。問句也可用 `when` 引導。如文中的 `today, when technology has taken the lead in modern romance`。例句：Many young people now use Threads, where they can freely express their thoughts.
