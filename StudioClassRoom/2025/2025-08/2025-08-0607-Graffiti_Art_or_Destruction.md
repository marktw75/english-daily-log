# Graffiti: Art or Destruction?

Is it OK to paint on surfaces in public places?

by Linda Ludwig

Coworkers Liam and Sophie are taking a walk during their lunch break.

Liam: Whoa, check out the graffiti on the side of that bridge! That's seriously impressive. The colors and design really brighten up this area.

Sophie: Yeah, it looks cool, but isn't it just vandalism? That's illegal. If someone painted that on your house, you wouldn't call it art, would you?

Liam: Probably not, and I get what you're saying, but graffiti is a form of expression. It can tell a story, make a statement or even make a dull space colorful.

Sophie: I know. Some scenes are really interesting. But some people just tag their name on a blank wall or someone else's painting. A lot of graffiti writers want nothing more than to gain recognition within the graffiti community for their specific tags.

Liam: I'll admit that sometimes graffiti doesn't seem to serve any purpose and is definitely ugly. It can actually be very negative. I read that gangs sometimes tag a wall to mark an area as theirs, so people don't feel safe. Also, some graffiti is just messy. It ruins public spaces, and cities have to spend millions of dollars cleaning it up.

Liam: That's fair. But graffiti can also be street art. Think about artists like Banksy - his work makes people think. Graffiti can challenge authority, spark conversations and even make communities more beautiful.

Sophie: So why not do it legally? Lots of places have designated areas for street art. Artists can use those walls instead of spraying paint on private property.

Liam: Some graffiti artists do seek legal ways to create their artwork. But not everyone is given permission. Someone using a blank wall of an old building to create something that looks cool should be celebrated.

Sophie: I get that, but there's a fine line between creating art and defacing property. The location, quality and message of good graffiti take planning. In some places it has even attracted visitors and boosted local economies.

Sophie: I never thought about it in that way. But sometimes the message is just meant to disturb people's way of thinking.

Liam: Well, that's another power of graffiti - it forces people to pay attention. And sometimes that is the only way someone can get their message across.

Sophie: Well, it's a very complex issue for sure.

Liam: That we can agree on!

## Usage Tip
### Tip 1
`If + S + Ved, S + would/could/might + ...`

本句型為「與現在事實相反」的假設語氣，前半句敘述與真實情況相反的假設，後半句則用來表示在這樣的假設情況下，可能會有什麼結果。`If + S + Ved, ...` 這種假設語氣表達出動作並非真的發生。Why not? 也可單獨使用，後面不加 `to`，表達「為何不？」。例句：A: Let's have dinner together after work. B: Why not?

### Tip 2
`Why not ...?`

可用來表示提議、建議或反問，語氣比直接命令柔和。後面通常接原形動詞或完整子句。文中的 `So why not do it legally?` 就是在提出建議：既然可以合法創作，為什麼不選擇合法的方式？
