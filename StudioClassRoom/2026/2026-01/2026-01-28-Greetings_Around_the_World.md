## Greetings Around the world

Seven ways people in other countries welcome one another

  

From Chile to Cambodia, many cultures have interesting ways of saying "Hello." Greetings run the gamut from air kisses to nose bumps. Greetings reflect cultural values - like respect, friendship or humility - and help people feel connected.

  

In Oman and the United Arab Emirates a greeting consists of touching noses while shaking hands. In New Zealand, the Indigenous people press their noses and heads together while breathing in one another's breath for a traditional greeting.

  

Air kisses are a popular from of welcome in Chile, Portugal, Spain and France, among others.

  

Mano po is a greeting in the Philippines that shows respect toward one's elder. This is done by placing the back of an older person's hand on the front of one's head.

  

The preference in many Western countries for a proper greeting is a firm handshake. And fist-bump greetings are common among the younger generation today. Yet people in some other cultures prefer greeting one another without touching. In Thailand, India, Nepal, Cambodia, Laos and Japan, for example, people are welcomed with a bow.

  

No matter how the welcome is performed, physical greetings allow people to convey thoughts and feelings with gestures that often speak louder than words.

  

### How People Bow in Different Countries

Thailand

A wai is when people press their palms together (like praying) at chest level and bow their heads slightly.

The higher the hands are held, the more respectful the gesture.

  

South Korea

A deep bow called jeol is used for elders and on special occasions.

People kneel and bow until theirforeheads touch the ground.

  

India

Namaste is the traditional greeting of pressing hands together in front of the chest with a slight bow.

It shows respect and humility and is used in both casual and formal settings.
