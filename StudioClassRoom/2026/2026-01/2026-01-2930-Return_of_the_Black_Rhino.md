# Return of the Black Rhino

Once nearly extinct, these creatures once again roam East Africa

  

Black rhinos were once early extinct, but there are now enough of them to open new rhino sanctuaries in places where rhinos have not lived for years.

  

After decades of decline, black rhinos in Kenya have begun a remarkable return, offering new hop for one of Africa's most endangered animals. Thanks to the efforts of conservation organizations, they are making a comeback in the grasslands of northern Kenya.

  

Black rhinos keep the East African ecosystem in balance by consuming large amounts of plant material. They eat enough plants to shape the land, providing habitats for several other species. For hunderds of years, these majestic creatures have captured the imaginations of humans, but this has also made them a target. Poachers have hunted rhinos for their horns for centuries, seeing them as symbol of power. Rhino horn is also used in Chinese medicine, although there is no scientific evidence that it promotes healing. This poaching, combined with the expansion of human settlements that destroy the rhinos' habitat, shrank the population of the black rhino until the species was on the brink of disappearing. At the worst point, only about 2,400 black rhinos remained, compared to an estimated 100,000 at the beginning fo the 20th century.

  

However, conservation organizations have succeeded in bringing back the black rhino population. As of 2025, there are now about 6,400 of them across Africa, and they being reintroduced to areas where they have not lived for decades.

  

The Kenya Wildlife Service has quadrupled the number of black rhinos living in Kenya since 1984. The main strategy has been to designate areas as rhino sanctuaries. In these sanctuaries the rhinos' habitat is protected, and the sanctuary can implement security measures to prevent poaching. They have been so successful that the rhino population in some sanctuaries grew until the rhinos began fighting over food and territory. So opening new sanctuaries to prevent these confrontations and let the population continue growing became necessary.

  

The newest sanctuary, Loisaba Conservancy, is the 17th rhino sanctuary opened in Kenya. It has been a wildlife conservancy for over a decade, but until 2024, no rhinos lived there since they had disappeared from the rigion 50 years before. Experts chose a group of rhinos to bring to the conservancy, selecting them largely for their potential for healthy offspring. Returning the rhinos to this location marked a triumph for conservation efforts.

  

Black rhinos are still critically endangered, so the conservation efforts still have a long way to go. If ongoing protection were to stop, their numbers would likely fall again -and they could even go extinct. Still, the return of black rhinos to Loisaba Conservancy shows that efforts to protect endangered species do make a difference.

### About Black Rhinos

Black rhinos stand about 1.6 meters tail at the shoulder, with males weighing up to 1,350 kilograms and females up to 900 kilograms. They tend to be solitary but do sometimes congregate in groups when environmental conditions are right. They have poor eyesight but strong senses of smell and hearing, which help them navigate their world.
