# More Than a Meal: How You Eat Matters

Is eating alone bad for you?

  

When I was growing up, my family shared a home-cooked dinner every night. We'd catch up on each other's days and make plans. Scarcely a dinner went by without a guest joining us, adding to the fun. I now prioritize dinnertime with my own family. Setting cellphones aside, we strengthen our connection over a meal. Studies confirm that shared family meals have physical and emotional benefits for children and youth. It reduces unhealthy eating habits, eating disorders, substance use and depression. The benefits of communal eating aren't just confined to family meals. A University of Oxford study found that people who place an emphasis on eating with others tend to feel happier and less alone. They feel better connected to their community and experience greater life satisfaction than those who usually eat alone.

  

Yet, modern culture has shifted away from communal meals and toward eating alone as people move farther from home, live alone and work at jobs with varying hours. According to Psychology TOday, Americans are eating nearly half of their meals alone! East Asian countries like Japan and Korea are also experiencing this trend toward eating solo.

  

Does this mean there are no benefits to solo dining? Not at all! While communal dining has health benefits, there are positive aspects to eating along as well. For some, eating alone helps them recharge and reduce stress after being in a socially demanding environment all day. There is also more personal choice and independence when eating alone with no need to consider others' food needs or preferences. To get the full benefits of eating alone, distractions must be set aside. Without conversation or digital screens capturing your attention, the flavors smells and textures of the food become more enjoyable. Quiet moments of eating with nothing else to distract you can also spark creativity. Rarely does a good idea become apparent to a person without the brain having the time and freedom to wander. Quietly eating a meal is an opportunity for this.

  

Eating with others and dining alone both have value. Shared meals build connection, while solo meals offer space to recharge. The key is being present. So, the next time you sit down to ear, turn off your screen, slow down and enjoy the moment - whether you're with other people or on your own.
