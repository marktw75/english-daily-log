# Bridging the Gap Between Askers and Guessers

Strategies for improving cross-cultural interactions
by Christine Bedwell

## Article

As Grace and Allison settled into Allison's room to study, Allison asked, "Do you want anything to eat or drink?"

"No, thanks," Grace replied politely, expecting Allison to extend the offer a second time as was customary in her experience.

Moments later, Allison returned with a drink for herself but nothing for Grace. Allison neither repeated her offer nor suggested sharing her own drink. Grace left Allison's house feeling thirsty, upset and neglected.

This incident illustrates a classic clash between Ask and Guess Cultures. Allison is part of the Ask Culture, which emphasizes direct, verbal communication and assumes people will state their needs clearly. Allison, an Asker, tends to ask for what she wants directly, confident that people will respond with "yes" if they are willing or able and "no" if not. In contrast, Grace grew up in the Guess Culture, where people avoid making direct requests, not wanting to impose or put people on the spot. And although in this culture, one might ask for something when the answer is a guaranteed "yes," Grace, a Guesser, is accustomed to saying no first before accepting food or drink. 

Guess Culture members are good at reading between the lines. They show their care for others by perceiving their needs. From Grace's perspective, Allison should have offered her a drink again or simply brought her one, guessing Grace was thirsty.

Communication styles of the Ask and Guess Cultures are common across the world. Most people of Asian, Middle Eastern and African heritage come from Guess Cultures, while Europeans and North Americans are more likely to belong to Ask Culture. Note, though, that all societies have elements of both cultures and that cultural background alone does not determine a person's communication style.

Having awareness of these communication differences is an asset in today's globalized world. By understanding Ask Culture and Guess Culture, respecting the differences and being flexible, you can navigate interactions with ease, setting yourself up for success.

First, figure out if you're an Asker or a Guesser. Then establish that you have different communication styles and make light of it, so no one is offended. Next, adapt your approach accordingly. Guessers can learn to accept Askers' direct style as sincere rather than pushy, while Askers can avoid putting pressure on Guessers by framing requests in ways that allow a graceful refusal. Guessers can also practice being more direct, while Askers can pay attention to hints and body language. If misunderstandings arise, both sides need to give each other the benefit of the doubt and be respectful.

In the end, it's not about whether Ask Culture or Guess Culture is right or wrong, it's about respecting each other's differences.

## Key Points

- In an Ask Culture, Askers rely on direct communication to ask for things, while in a Guess Culture, Guessers are accustomed to paying attention to hints and body language.
- Understanding the differences between Ask and Guess Culture and learning to respect your communication style will help prevent culture shock.

## Usage Tip

### Tip 1
S + neither A nor B

用來表示「既不……也不……」。neither 與 nor 會一起出現，用來連接兩個被否定的對等語意。例如：
The food served at the party was neither delicious nor healthy. 
宴會上的食物既不好吃也不健康。

### Tip 2
It's not about A, it's about B.

用來強調真正重點不在 A，而在 B。例如：
It's not about winning the argument; it's about finding the best solution. 
重點不在於贏得爭論，而在於找到最好的解決方案。
