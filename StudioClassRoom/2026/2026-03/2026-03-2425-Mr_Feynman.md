# Surely You're Joking, Mr. Feynman
A great scientist's life story in his own words

Curiosity and courage enabled physicist Richard Feynman to accomplish amazing things. One of his many discoveries related to quantum mechanics won him the Nobel Prize. He also worked on the team that developed the atomic bomb. Feynman also enjoyed telling stories, and many of his best stories were collected in a book titled Surely you're Joking, Mr. Feynman.

Feynman told the stories in the book to a friend, who wrote them down, giving the book a fun and lively tone. Although the details of quantum mechanics are beyond most people's knowledge, Feynman's stories are accessible and funny.

In the midst of his anecdotes, Feynman also conveys some life lessons that led him to success. Feynman's life was marked by curiosity. It motivated his research into physics but extended beyond his one field. He also learned music and drawing and even developed some skill in picking locks.

Many people make decisions based on what they think others will think of them, but Feynman encouraged people to not car about others' opinions. He thought people should live their lives the way they wanted to without worrying about what other people thought.

Feynman considered honesty very important, including honesty with oneself. He wrote, "The first principle is that you must not fool yourself - and you are the easiest person to fool." He added that people should avoid fooling others by honestly reporting both their theories and all the evidence for and against them.

Feynman's concern for honesty paid off when he served on a committee investigating the explosion of the Challenger spacecraft back in 1986. Feynman found the mechanical problem that led to the explosion. And he also discovered that others had noticed the problem, but their concerns had been covered up for political reasons. Feynman explained the problem live on national TV, which meant that it would be taken seriously.

This focus on honesty is important in other situation too. Instead of trying to convince yourself that your first thought is right, make a point of looking at things honestly - even if you may not like what you see. Only when you look at a problem honestly can you work out a solution to it.

The book's emphasis on humor and stories emphasize the timeless lesson that honestly - both in science and in life - is the key to finding real solutions.
